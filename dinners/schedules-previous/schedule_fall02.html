This Semester's Schedule

  1. Wednesday, November 20, 2002 (6:00pm)
    The Dual Corporate Ladder and New Hires in the Corporate World
    Professor Thomas Allen, Howard W. Johnson Professor of Management

    Prof Allen will talk about the dual corporate ladder, which has been used by many companies to recruit engineers. He will discuss whether the dual corporate ladder really exists, if it is actually effective, and its implications for engineers in corporations. He will also discuss the role, expectations, treatment, and training of new hires in the corporate world and the way these things affect the employees and the company.

    Prof Allen specializes in organizational psychology and management. He explores the relationship between organizational structure and behavior, the role of technological gatekeepers in technology transfer, and the influence of architectural layout on communication. Prof Allen also examines international technology transfer, reward systems for technical professionals, and the impact of organizational structure on project performance. His long-term research targets the pharmaceutical and aerospace industries, addressing principally the issue of project management.

    Register by November 16, 2002.

  2. Friday, October 11, 2002 (6:00pm)
    Examples of Academia / Industry Interface and Nanotechnology
    Professor Robert Langer, Kenneth J. Germeshausen Professor of Chemical and Biomedical Engineering

    Dr. Langer is the Chairman of the United States Food and Drug Administration's SCIENCE Board, the FDA's highest advisory board. Dr. Langer has written over 700 articles and 420 abstracts. He has also written over 400 patents, one of which was cited as the outstanding patent in Massachusetts in 1988 and one of 20 outstanding patents in the United States. Dr. Langer's patents have been licensed or sublicensed to over 80 pharmaceutical, chemical, biotechnology and medical device companies; a number of these companies were launched on the basis of these patent licenses. Dr. Langer has received over over 100 major awards. He is the only engineer to receive the Gairdner Foundation International Award; 56 recipients of this award have subsequently received a Nobel Prize, and he received the $500,00.00 Lemelson-MIT prize, the world's largest prize for invention for being "one of history's most prolific inventors in medicine." In 2002, he received the $500,000 Charles Stark Draper Prize, the world's most prestigious engineering prize, from the National Academy of Engineers. In 1989 Dr. Langer was elected to the Institute of Medicine of the National Academy of Sciences, land in 1992 he was elected to both the National Academy of Engineering and to the National Academy of Sciences. He is the only active member of all three United States National Academies.

    Forbes Magazine (1999) and BioWorld (1990) have named Langer as one of the 25 most important individuals in biotechnology in the world. Discover Magazine (2002) named him as one of the 20 most important people in this area. Time Magazine and CNN (2001) named Langer as one of the 100 most important people in America and one of the 18 top people in science or medicine in America. He has served, at various times, on 8 boards of directors and 20 Scientific Advisory Boards of such companies as Alkermes, Mitsubishi Pharmaceuticals, Warner-Lambert, Guilford Pharmaceuticals. He received his Bachelor's Degree from Cornell University in 1970 and his Sc.D. from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 1974, both in chemical engineering.

  3. Wednesday, September 25, 2002 (6:00pm)
    Presentation of Photo-Journal Documenting a 2-Week Trip to Bali, Indonesia
    Samuel Jay Keyser, MIT Peter de Florez Professor Emeritus of Linguistics
    joined by his spouse, Nancy Kelly

    Join us as Professor Keyser and his wife present the photographic record of their trip to Bali, Indonesia this past April.

    Professor Keyser is editor-in-chief of Linguistic Inquiry, a major journal in the field of theoretical linguistics as well as an associated monograph and book series. Jay has published one books of poem and an children's book, On the Horizon, is scheduled for publication in the Fall of next year. An excerpt from his Malawi/Zambia safari journal appeared in the May 2000 issue of "The Atlantic Monthly."

    Professor Keyser will be joined by his spouse Nancy Kelly. Nancy is an inveterate traveler and an accomplished photographer who has recorded several trips with Jay. She has been a member of the MIT Office of the President for three decades, currently as Administrative Officer. Below are her comments about her two passions - travel and photography:

    "I must have been born with a travel gene. I remember dreaming about exploring different parts of the world by the time I was eight years old. That was the year my mother began managing a seasonal inn on the coast of North Carolina, my home state. Each summer, guests came from around the country and different parts of the world, and I couldn't wait to quiz them about where they were from, how far away it was, and what it was like there. I began compiling a mental list of places to explore, one that grew longer each summer.

    "I have been traveling ever since, with a camera as my constant companion. I love to observe people, places, objects, animals, most everything through a camera lens and record them on film. It helps me see more clearly and differently. I discover patterns and shapes that aren't there if I'm cameraless. I capture for a lifetime smiles, looks, and events that last an instant. Best of all, when I've forgotten some small detail or precious moment that made an adventure special, all I have to do is look back through my photographs to bring the memories flooding back to the surface so I can relive the experience all over again.

    "Photography is the way I travel when the demands of daily living keep me inside, looking out the window and wondering when the signs will be favorable for my next adventure. My exploration list continues to grow each year."
