  1. Wednesday, September 22, 1999 (6:00pm, Room 6-120)
    This is the opening lecture of the Spring 1999 series of dinners. As such, refreshments will be served instead of the full course dinner. This lecture will be open to everyone.
    Drug Discovery - From Serendipity to Rational Design in the Hands of Chemists
    “From Chinese herbal medicines to modern antibiotics and anticancer drugs, serendipity has played a key role in identifying pharmaceuticals. But only one in ten-thousand compounds evaluated by random screening ever makes it through clinical trials. Can we do better? This seminar addresses the question by examining the case study of cisplatin, a platinum-containing anticancer drug that has virtually eradicated testicular cancer and is showing great promise to do the same for cervical cancer. The desire to design drugs by rational methods has driven chemists for decades to probe the workings of the cell and use the power of synthesis to help improve the human condition. It would appear that we are getting close, as modern chemistry provides the tools to make the dream a reality. ” by Stephen Lippard, Chemistry Department Head, and Amos Noyes, Professor of Chemistry.

    The Remarkable Properties of Carbon Nanotubes
    “ A brief overview, interspersed with anecdotes, will be given of the remarkable structural and electronic properties of carbon nanotubes, which are tiny structures of molecular dimensions in the form of hollow cylinders with about 20 carbon atoms around the circumference of the cylinders. The unique electronic properties of these carbon nanotubes are that they can be either semiconducting or metallic depending on their geometry. Potential practical electronic applications of nanotubes will also be considered. ” by Mildred Dresselhaus, Institute Professor of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science and Institute Professor of Physics.

  8. February 17, 2000 (6:00pm, Ashdown West Dining Room)
    Myth-making and the State in Medieval and Renaissance Venice
    “This illustrated and interactive talk will be about the use of myth to enhance a state's power. Although my extended example will be medieval and renaissance venice, the story applies to most states, including the US. We will examine how myth is elaborated, and then how it is used by the state to perpetuate itself, to impress others, and to maintain control over its citizens. Finally we will see if we can draw any useful conclusions from the venetian story for our own situation.” by Arthur Steinber, Professor of Anthropology and Director of ISP.
