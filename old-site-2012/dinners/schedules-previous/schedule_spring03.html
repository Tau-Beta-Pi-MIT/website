This Semester's Schedule

  1. Thursday, April 3, 2003
    Topic: Cosmic Inflation and the Accelerating Universe
    Professor Alan Guth, V F Weisskopf Professor Of Physics

    Inflationary cosmology offers possible explanations for many features of our universe, including its uniformity, its mass density, and the faint ripples that are now being observed in the cosmic background radiation. The recently discovered acceleration of the universe has radically altered our picture of the universe, but has also helped to confirm the basic predictions of inflation.

    ALAN H. GUTH is the Victor F. Weisskopf Professor of Physics and a Margaret MacVicar Faculty Fellow at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. Trained in particle theory at MIT, Guth held postdoctoral positions at Princeton, Columbia, Cornell, and SLAC before returning to MIT as a faculty member in 1980. His work in cosmology began at Cornell, when Henry Tye persuaded him to study the production of magnetic monopoles in the early universe. Using standard assumptions, they found that far too many would be produced. Continuing this work at SLAC, Guth discovered that the magnetic monopole glut could be avoided by a new proposal which he called the inflationary universe. Guth is a member of the National Academy of Sciences and the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, and had been awarded the Franklin Medal for Physics of the Franklin Institute and the Dirac Prize of the International Center for Theoretical Physics. Guth is still busy exploring the consequences of inflation. He has also written a popular-level book called "The Inflationary Universe: The Quest for a New Theory of Cosmic Origins" (Addison-Wesley/Perseus Books, 1997).

  2. Monday, May 5, 2003
    Topic: Depression
    Professor Eric Feron, Department of Aeronautics and Astronautics

    Depression is an illness that can considerably affect an individual's mind and spirit. As such, the MIT community, and MIT students in particular, can suffer considerably from the effects of depression, since it touches what makes us strong in the first place. In this talk/discussion, I will present a candid view of depression, its symptoms, its consequences on personal and professional life, and how to get out of it. I will draw from my own experience to describe these various points. I will also try and give practical advice about how depression can be dealt with effectively.

    ERIC FERON is an Associate Professor of Aeronautics and Astronautics, at MIT since 1993. He is the happy father of three kids and busy advisor to an unknown but large number of undergraduate and graduate students. His hobbies include collecting old science books and XVIIIth century French furniture, sailing and skiing.
